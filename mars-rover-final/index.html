About the Mars Rover Program

NASA’s Mars Rover Program uses advanced robotic vehicles to investigate Mars’ geology, climate, atmosphere, and potential for ancient life. Rovers such as Curiosity and Perseverance examine Martian rocks, soil, and environmental conditions to uncover the planet’s past and support future human missions.

Learn more at NASA’s official Mars Exploration Program: mars.nasa.gov.

Featured Rovers

Curiosity

Mission Goals & Overview

Curiosity investigates whether Mars ever had conditions suitable for microbial life. Its research focuses on water activity, geology, climate, and environmental history.

Launch & Landing

Curiosity launched on November 26, 2011, and landed in Gale Crater on August 6, 2012 using the innovative Sky Crane landing system.

Major Discoveries

The rover discovered ancient lakebeds, organic molecules, and methane fluctuations—key evidence that Mars once offered habitable conditions.

Key Technical Features

Curiosity includes a drill, robotic arm, Mastcam, ChemCam laser, radiation detector, and onboard laboratory instruments for analyzing Martian samples.

Perseverance

Mission Goals & Overview

Perseverance searches for signs of ancient microbial life, collects rock samples for potential return to Earth, and tests new technology for future human exploration.

Launch & Landing

Launched July 30, 2020, Perseverance touched down in Jezero Crater on February 18, 2021—an area rich in geological clues about ancient water.

Major Discoveries

The rover has documented sedimentary structures, minerals, and features that point to past habitability and long-standing water systems.

Key Technical Features

Perseverance is equipped with spectrometers, ground-penetrating radar, upgraded wheels, a sample caching system, and microphones that captured the first sounds from another planet.

Ingenuity Helicopter

Ingenuity achieved the first powered flight on another planet, proving that aerial scouting is possible on Mars.
